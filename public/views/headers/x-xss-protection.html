Sure, of course you have made it impossible for cross site scripting (XSS) attacks to take place on your site. Of course you have!

That's what they all say.

The X-XSS-Protection header comes to us from Microsoft and is used by Internet Explorer (IE). IE's built-in ability to stop XSS attacks from harming users has improved over time. Lots of XSS protections are in place already by default in IE.

However if you would like to tell IE to be even more strict about how it responds when it suspects an XSS attack, you can use the X-XSS-Protection to do so. There are multiple settings you can use with X-XSS-Protection. The one we like the best is shown in the example above, and outright prevents IE from rendering a page that it suspects may be loading an XSS attack.

Other X-XSS-Protection options are to sanitize the content and then render it, or to render the content even though it looks like an attack. We don't recommend either of these options. Better safe than sorry, right?
