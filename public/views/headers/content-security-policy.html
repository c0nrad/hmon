Your best defense against Cross Site Scripting (XSS) What it looks like: Content-Security-Policy: script-src 'self' https://apis.google.com

A great example: Simplemachines.org

Tell Me More

Content Security Policy (CSP) prevents cross site scripting by explicitly declaring to browsers which script, media, stylesheets, etc are supposed to be run from your website. By whitelisting these resources, if an attacker is ever able to embed his evil code on your site, the browser will ignore it and visitors to your site will remain safe.

The types of things you can whitelist using CSP include:

  • script so javascript from a bad guy's site won't load
  • images
  • frames, like those offered by YouTube
  • stylesheets
  • media like audio and video files
  • fonts


Johannes Ullrich of SANS believes that CSP is absolutely recommended when creating a new website but in the June 13, 2013 edition of his Internet Storm Center StormCast he states that CSP is "not easy to implement on existing websites that weren't really coded around it".

Not easy but very worthwhile!

Start Small

Don't feel like you need to tackle every possible CSP directive the first time you use this header. When you begin implementing this header, just mention a single resource, like frames. Then give it a week and if it looks like nothing on your site broke, add another directive in your CSP header like image. As you go, you'll build confidence and your site becomes safer for visitors, which is good for business.

Content Security Policy is perhaps the most powerful security header you can use on your website. Go for it, and let us know how it goes!
